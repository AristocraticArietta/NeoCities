Most couture collections come and go without much of a trace. They might be known for a few looks, but rarely is the whole collection remembered. However, Thierry Mugler's fall 1995 haute couture collection is an exception. It's a collection that has stuck with me since I first saw it, and I think it's a collection that deserves to be remembered. This collection takes inspiration from the architectural silhouettes of the 1940s and the futuristic sleekness of the 1990s, creating a collection that is both timeless and modern. It's a collection that is both beautiful and iconic, and I think it will go down as one of the greatest collections of all time. Its legacy is unparaelled and it has been the foundation for many later collections by other designers.

In this post, I want to take a deep dive into this collection and explore what makes it so special. We'll look at the silhouettes, the fabrics, the inspiration, and the overall message of the collection. I have curated a handful of looks from this collection, but know that there are many more looks that I did not cover here. Should you wish to view the collection in full, head over to [Vogue Runway's Image Archive](https://www.vogue.com/fashion-shows/image-archive) to view the whole collection. You will need a subscription to Vogue Magazine to view the whole collection.

## Look 4 - Worn by Simonetta Gianfelici
<Image src={look3} alt="A woman dressed in a black lace bodysuit and black latex corset with a black and white half-mask. She is in the middle of throwing off her black latex coat, which pools around her." />

Mugler often used suprising materials in his work, often going for plastics, rubber, and other synthetic materials. This image shows his mastery over latex, using it in corsetry and to make a big coat that the model dramatically throws off, revealing a lace bodysuit and the latex corset underneath, creating two looks for the price of one. Her mask is also a source of intrigue, as it covers half her face and twists up above her head. Overall, this look is about the proverbial masks we wear: What we wear allows us to put on masks to fit into certain situations, but we may be a completely different person underneath. In this case, the coat and mask suggest that we hide behind society's rules, when underneath we are something much more free and unique.

## Look 27 - Worn by Nadja Auermann
<Image src={look27} alt="A woman wears a structured white suit jacket and skirt while her large coat hangs from her wrists and falls behind her." />

This is a classic Mugler look. The 1940s-esque tailored suit, the faux fur around the cuffs of the sleeves, the large coat, and the pearl pin. Mugler was renowned for his tailoring skills, and this look is an excellent example of that. This look is very feminine while also having elements of androgyny through the tailored suit and large coat, which feels very 90's to me. This is important to note, as women in the 90's were often trying to be seen as equals to men, and were blurring the lines between masculine and feminine fashion, especially in the workplace. Mugler takes this concept and elevates it to haute couture, creating a look that is both beautiful and empowering.

## Look 32 - Worn by Helena Barquilla
<Image src={look32} alt="A woman wears a structured black suit jacket, complete with white shirt and black tie. She wears a tight-fitting pencil skirt and carries a briefcase. Her teasing expression suggests that she may not be the typical businesswoman." />

Similar to the last look, this look also plays on the idea of androgyny and the blurring of gender lines in the 1990s. The white shirt and black tie are traditional symbols of masculinity, but Mugler subverts expectations by pairing them with a tight-fitting pencil skirt and a playful expression. The briefcase adds a touch of realism to the look, grounding it in the world of business and finance. This also plays into the idea of feminism, as it challenges traditional gender roles and expectations.

## Look 38 - Worn by Simonetta Gianfelici
<Image src={look38} alt="A woman wears a structured red shirt with matching velvet pencil skirt. She drags a black opera coat behind her that has a boa feather trim. She has a red hairband." />

This look is #tailoring-goals. The way the shirt perfectly fits her body is amazing, and the many different panels of the shirt can be seen in this photo. It is definitely straight out of the 1940s, especially with the closure at the neck. The black opera coat adds drama. The different textures of the outfit - the velvet pencil skirt, the silk shirt, and the boa feathers on the coat - work well together to add interest to the outfit. 

## Look 43/44 - Worn by Nadja Auermann
<Image src={look43} alt="A woman wearing a purple opera cape holds it open, revealing a black slip. Her head is covered in metal, while her face is covered in clear plastic." />
<Image src={look44} alt="A woman in a cyborg-esque silver bodysuit made of metal and clear plastic. The metal covers most of her body, while the clear plastic reveals the stomach, buttocks, breasts, and face." />

This look is perhaps the best known from the collection, as Zendaya wore "Robot couture" to the premiere of *Dune: Part Two* in 2024, which promptly broke the internet. Created at a time when the intersection between technology such as the Internet and everyday life was becoming more and more blurred, this outfit is a representation of how we have become one with our technology, and how our lives are now inextricably linked with it. This look challenges the distinction between man and machine, and questions what it means to be human in a world increasingly dominated by technology. The themes of this look are very relevant today, and I think that's why it continues to resonate with people. Inspired by Fritz Lang's groundbreaking 1927 film *Metropolis* and the artwork of Hajime Sorayama, the look also has feminist themes. The look can be seen as a figure of strength and beauty, but also as a critique on the objectification of the female body. In terms of craftsmanship, this look was created using plastic (perspex) and metal, and featured the talents of not only Mugler but also Mr. Pearl, Jean-Jacques Urcun, and Jean-Pierre Delcros. 

## Look 73 - Worn by Julie Newmar
<Image src={look73} alt="A woman wears a purple cape lined with black silk, revealing her white bodysuit with black stripes akin to a zebra. The stripes follow the contours of her body and the bodysuit features jewel embellishments."/>

This is a great example of Mugler's use of animal prints and patterns in his work. The white bodysuit with black stripes is reminiscent of a zebra, and the purple cape and jewel embellishments add a touch of drama and luxury. This exploration of the blurring between human and animal can be taken to have many meanings. I am of the mind that this look says that humans are animals deep down and that we are all the same deep down, no matter what labels society tries to put on us. This is true for women especially, as women are expected to be refined, demure, and tame - the opposite of an animal. Mugler is setting free the inner animal inside women through this garment.

## Look 79 - Worn by Helena Barquilla
<Image src={look79} alt="A woman stands in a bodysuit with leopard spots printed over it, while she has a peacock-like tail made of leopard print panels trimmed with boa feathers. Jewels cover her chest, neck, and lower torso."/>

Another great example of Mugler's use of animal prints and patterns in his work. In this case, the leopard and the peacock. He juxtaposes the two animals, one known for its beauty and one known for its fierceness. Mugler suggests that the two animals coexist inside people, and can be combined to be a thing of beauty.

## Look 83 - Worn by Marie Helvin
<Image src={look83} alt="A woman stands in a gold lame dress with red jewels around her neck and in her hair. The dress is reminiscent of ancient Greek goddesses, and features cutouts around her sides and back."/>

This look serves to make women a thing of worship. I see a clear allusion to Ancient Greek goddesses in the gold gown and jeweled accessories. This allows women to reclaim their power as goddesses, especially in a world that often seeks to diminish them. By draping his model in gold, Mugler is suggesting that women are precious and valuable, and should be revered as such. 

## Look 90 - Worn by Elle Macpherson
<Image src={look90} alt="A woman wears a colorful skirt and top combination, where shiny sequins in many different colors are arranged in sunbursts. Only the underside of the skirt is embellished."/>

This look is so fun and playful! It is a celebration of color and movement. The colorful sequins are very cheerful, and the way the sequins on the underside of the skirt catch the light as she walks is beautiful. It feels like a celebration of femininity in a way that is not objectifying or sexualizing. It's just pure joy.

## Look 99 - Worn by Simonetta Gianfelici
<Image src={look99} alt=" A woman wears a nude bodysuit on top of a nude corset. Around her waist and neck are multiple strands of pearls. Her skirt is a long black pencil skirt that instead of ending at the waist, it extends out from her sides and encircles her, creating a fan shape. The interior of the fan is a blush pink, and she wears matching gloves. The look is akin to an shell and its pearls."/>

The first thing I noticed from this look is the obvious inspiration from the sea. The fan around her is reminiscent of a shell, which makes the model the pearl. To strengthen this effect, the model wears a lot of pearls, both on her hips and her neck. Mugler probably is suggesting that women should be valued more, like the pearls they are. This look is also just plain beautiful, as well as an amazing example of Mugler's innovative techniques.

## Look 101 - Worn by Nadja Auermann
<Image src={look101} alt="A woman wears a gold metal corset that covers her lower torso and matching opera gloves. The look is futuristic, and the corset is embellished with white gemstones. She wears a large necklace made of the same gemstones."/>

Mugler really liked to blend technology and fashion, as seen here again. This look reminds me of a mix between a cyborg and a knight's armor. It suggests that women are strong beings who do not have to compromise their femininity in order to be powerful. The white gemstones on the corset as well as the large necklace are likely meant to evoke a more feminine and delicate image, but Mugler probably chose white gemstones as they resemble diamonds, the toughest material on earth, to further suggest how strong women are.

## Look 102 - Worn by Jerry Hall
<Image src={look102} alt="A woman wears a floor-length beaded gown as well as a humungous white faux fur coat. The coat has an ombre effect towards the hem and the cuffs, turning purple."/>

This look is one of my personal favorites, just because it's so glamorous and dramatic. The beaded gown is stunning, and the faux fur coat is so luxurious. I would love to wear this look to a fancy event. It's just so extra, in the best way possible. Here, Mugler probably wanted to celebrate the dramatic and extra side of women, which is often seen as a negative trait by many. This gown/coat combination is the perfect example of going against the grain and showing the world that women can be both glamorous and dramatic.

## Look 104 - Worn by Eva Herzigova
<Image src={look104} alt="A woman wears a red beaded corset gown with matching red cape. A large feathered boa, also in a matching shade of red, is draped around her neck and extends over her arms."/>

This look is so extra and I love it! The insane feather boa and the red color remind me of those male birds, who live in South America, that lift their wings in a fan shape when they are trying to defend a mate from other males. The red color says "Stay away!" and the boa compounds this idea. This look enables a woman to be dangerous and take up space, which is the opposite of what society tells women they should be.

## Look 105 - Worn by Naomi Campbell
<Image src={look105} alt="A woman wears a sheer black bodysuit decorated with leopard print. She wears a floor-length sheer black cape as well, which is decorated with black and red feathers at the neckline and hem."/>

This look is another great example of Mugler's fascination with animal prints and patterns. The leopard print and the feathers evoke the image of a powerful predator, while the sheer black fabric of the cape adds a touch of mystery and allure. Mugler probably intended to make women feel powerful and confident in their own skin, and this look does just that.

## Look 107 - Worn by Kabuki Starshine
<Image src={look107} alt="A man in drag wears a white underbust corset that covers his lower torso, as well as a large, rigid, shell-shaped art piece made of wire and pearls attached to his hips."/>

Last but not least, this look is important to include because it challenges gender norms in fashion. Mugler was known for his hyper-masculine and hyper-feminine designs, but he also pushed boundaries by featuring men, transgender people, and gender non-conforming people in his womenswear shows. This look is a great example of that, and it's a reminder that fashion is for everyone, regardless of gender. Specifically, this look is reminiscent of Botticelli's *The Birth of Venus*, and it is a great example of Mugler's ability to blend classical art with modern fashion.
